### Coefficient of Variation

The coefficient of variation (CV = std dev / mean) quantifies dispersion relative to the mean. For word counts (positive integers), a CV above 100% indicates an extremely skewed or multimodal distribution.

MemOS has a CV of 112%: its standard deviation exceeds its mean. Table 4 confirms a bimodal pattern -- 297 answers fall at 0-1x gold length while 126 fall at 20x+, with the median (6.00) far below the mean (15.06). Mem0 (89%) and MemU (86%) show similarly high variance relative to their means.

AP v2 (deliberately wrong, vague-but-topical answers) has the tightest distribution of any system (CV = 42%) and scores 62.7% overall. Mem0 scores 64.2% with more than double the variance (CV = 89%). The 1.5-point gap spans 1,540 questions.

None of the published evaluations include confidence intervals or statistical significance tests for the reported accuracy differences.

| MemU      |            5.05 |        66.6% |       1.03 |
| Mem0      |            4.48 |        64.2% |       0.92 |

**Pearson r (word count vs. accuracy):** 0.6039
**Spearman rho (word count vs. accuracy):** 0.6429

Systems without the word-limit instruction produce longer answers and tend to score higher. The correlation is moderate (rho = 0.64), not perfect: the AP v2 baseline achieves 62.7% with only 6.5 mean words, while Mem0 achieves 64.2% with 4.5 mean words. Word count alone does not explain score differences; the interaction between answer length and the judge's "be generous" instruction is the mechanism. See [prompts.md](prompts.md) for the judge prompt analysis.

## Judge Leniency and the 99 Corrupted Questions

Higher-scoring systems receive a larger score boost from the judge's leniency on the 99 corrupted questions in the benchmark. From [results-audit/RESULTS_AUDIT.md](../results-audit/RESULTS_AUDIT.md):

| System | Correct on Corrupted (of 99) | Rate | Overall Accuracy |
|--------|------------------------------|------|-----------------|
| EverMemOS | 60 | 60.6% | 92.32% |
| Zep | 53 | 53.5% | 85.22% |
| MemU | 48 | 48.5% | 66.67% |
| MemOS | 47 | 47.5% | 80.76% |
| Mem0 | 34 | 34.3% | 64.20% |

The pattern: systems that produce longer, more verbose answers (EverMemOS, Zep) are more often marked correct on corrupted questions because their answers provide more surface area for the judge's generous semantic matching. This is consistent with the word-count/accuracy correlation above. The judge's "as long as it touches on the same topic" instruction compounds with answer length to inflate scores.

Source: [results-audit/RESULTS_AUDIT.md](../results-audit/RESULTS_AUDIT.md), "Judge Leniency on Corrupted Questions" section
